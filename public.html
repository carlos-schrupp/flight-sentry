Flight Sentry

A project that estimates the chance a flight will depart late, using information that would have been known two hours before takeoff.

In one sentence: Flight Sentry looks at patterns from past flights and weather to give an early “delay risk” signal—so people can plan before delays snowball.

Why flight delays are hard (and why early warning helps)

Delays can come from many sources at once: weather, congestion at busy airports, earlier flights arriving late, or operational constraints. Once delays begin, they can spread through the day.

Who benefits

  • Airlines/airports: plan gates, crews, and communications earlier.
  • Travelers: set expectations and plan backups sooner.
  • Operations teams: focus attention on flights most likely to slip.

What “2 hours before” means

The estimate is made using only information that would have been available before the flight—similar to how you’d check traffic and weather before leaving for the airport.

What goes into the estimate (plain English)

Flight Sentry does not “know the future”. It learns patterns from history and applies them to upcoming flights.

Examples of signals

  • How delayed flights have been recently at the origin airport
  • Time of day / day of week patterns (rush hours, weekends)
  • Weather near the airport around the cutoff time (2 hours before)
  • Knock-on effects: when planes arrive late, later departures can slip too

How it avoids “cheating”

  • It excludes any information that only becomes known after a flight departs.
  • It lines up weather correctly in time, rather than using later observations.
  • It tests on later time periods to simulate making predictions in the future.

What we learned (high level)

  • Delays are often “systemic”: they build up through congestion and knock-on effects, not just one cause.
  • Weather matters, but so do operational patterns (time of day and busy hubs).
  • To make a fair evaluation, you must test on later time periods and avoid using after-the-fact fields.

In our best overall configuration, the system could identify a large share of flights that eventually departed late in a later-year test set (not perfect—but useful as an early warning signal).

Limitations (what this can’t do)

  • It’s based on historical patterns; rare events can break patterns.
  • It’s an estimate, not a guarantee—airline operations change minute to minute.
  • Turning a project into a product would require live data feeds and ongoing monitoring.
